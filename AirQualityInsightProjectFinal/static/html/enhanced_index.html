üõë Causes of Air Pollution

  • Vehicular Emissions: Road transport is a major source of urban air pollution. Vehicles emit nitrogen oxides (NO‚Çì), carbon monoxide (CO), and particulate matter (PM). According to the World Health Organization, vehicle emissions are responsible for about 30% of PM2.5 levels in major cities. (Wikipedia)
  • Industrial Activity: Industries, especially in developing nations, release pollutants like sulfur dioxide (SO‚ÇÇ), nitrogen dioxide (NO‚ÇÇ), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs). These contribute to smog and acid rain. In India, industry accounts for about 21% of total air pollution emissions. (Wikipedia)
  • Construction and Road Dust: Unregulated construction releases fugitive dust, consisting of PM10 and PM2.5 particles. According to the Centre for Science and Environment (CSE), construction dust contributes to over 30% of PM2.5 pollution in Indian cities like Delhi. (Wikipedia)
  • Agricultural Practices: Stubble burning in northern India releases vast amounts of PM2.5, methane, and ammonia. The Indian Agricultural Research Institute reports that stubble burning contributes up to 35% of Delhi's pollution in peak winter months. (Wikipedia)
  • Waste Burning: Open burning of municipal and plastic waste emits toxic chemicals including black carbon, dioxins, and furans. Studies estimate this source contributes up to 11% of ambient PM2.5 levels in Indian cities. (Wikipedia)
  • Natural Causes: Dust storms, wildfires, and volcanic eruptions contribute to temporary spikes in air pollution. For instance, dust storms from the Thar Desert raise PM10 levels significantly during summer months across northwestern India. (Wikipedia)

üè• Health Impact

Air pollution poses a significant threat to global health, contributing to a wide range of serious health conditions.

  • Premature Deaths: According to the WHO, air pollution is responsible for around 6.7 million premature deaths annually, including 4.2 million from outdoor air pollution.
  • Cardiovascular & Respiratory Diseases: Prolonged exposure to pollutants like PM2.5 and NO‚ÇÇ increases the risk of heart disease, stroke, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and lung cancer.
  • Children's Health: Air pollution causes acute lower respiratory infections in children and impairs lung development, especially in urban areas with high traffic and industrial activity.
  • Pregnancy & Infant Health: Exposure to polluted air during pregnancy is linked to up to 6 million premature births and 3 million low-birth-weight babies globally each year.
  • Brain & Neurological Effects: Recent studies suggest that pollutants may enter the brain and contribute to neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's.
